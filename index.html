Part 1 — The Foundation

The One Idea That Makes Everything Click

Trading, at its core, is supply and demand. Nansen tells you who owns the supply, and who is the demand. That's it. Everything else is detail.

Sit with that for a moment, because it's the lens that makes everything in this playbook make sense. Every workflow, every post format, every content angle you'll learn here comes back to one question:

What's happening between the people who hold the tokens and the people who want them?

TLDR

Nansen shows you who holds the tokens (supply) and who's actively buying them (demand). That's your content edge in one sentence.

Why Blockchain Changes the Game

Most financial markets are hidden. You can see stock prices, but you can't see what Goldman Sachs bought this morning, or when a hedge fund started quietly building a position before everyone caught on.

Crypto is different. Every transaction is public, permanent, and traceable on the blockchain. Every wallet, every movement, every buy and sell is recorded and visible, to anyone who knows how to read it.

The problem is the noise. There are millions of wallets and billions of transactions. Without the right tools, finding what matters is like searching a library with no index.

Nansen is that index. It labels wallets, tracks fund flows, and surfaces what matters: who is moving money, where it's going, and what it typically means when they do.

Think about it this way: imagine you could see every trade Goldman Sachs made, in real time, before it showed up in any filing. That's what on-chain data gives you for crypto, and Nansen makes it readable.

Your Role as an Affiliate

Be clear on what your job actually is, because it's simpler than most people expect.

Most people have no idea where the money is in crypto, or where it's going. On-chain analysis that surfaces this typically takes years of experience to develop. Think of investigators like ZachXBT, well-known for tracing crypto wallets, following money flows, and uncovering what's really happening behind the scenes.

Nansen does the analytical heavy lifting for you. It identifies the wallets, labels the entities, and surfaces the movements.

Your job is to become the distribution channel. You don't need to be a trader or an analyst. You need to be the person who finds what Nansen surfaces and shares it with an audience that doesn't have the tools or time to look for themselves.

That's a genuinely valuable role, and it's one most people aren't filling yet.

Ask yourself: how many people in my timeline right now are posting on-chain data with real wallet labels and real numbers behind it? Probably very few. That's the gap you're filling.

TLDR

You don't need to trade. You need to find the signal in Nansen and share it with people who don't have the tools or time to look themselves. Nansen does the analysis. You do the distribution.

The Smart Money Hierarchy

Not all market participants are equal. Understanding who each player is shapes how you interpret what Nansen shows you.

Think of crypto token markets as having two sides: those who supply the tokens, and those who buy them.

When you see a wallet moving tokens in Nansen, the first question you should ask yourself is: which side of this hierarchy does this wallet sit on? That answer changes the entire story.

The Supply Side

These are the people who created the project and received tokens before anyone else could buy them.

PlayerWhat They DoContent Angle
FoundationsThe org behind the project. They hold a large portion at launch and control release.Foundation wallet moving large amounts unexpectedly = newsworthy.
VCsEarly investors. Tokens on a vesting schedule, released gradually.VC vesting ends + selling = downward pressure. Nansen shows this in real time.
FoundersBuilt the project. Token grants on a vesting schedule.Selling behaviour after vesting events = strong content signal.

The Demand Side

These are the people buying tokens on the open market.

PlayerWhat They DoContent Angle
Hedge FundsProfessional investment vehicles. Large, organised buyers.Large fund purchases signal institutional interest.
Market MakersEntities like Wintermute or Jump. Provide liquidity + sometimes take directional positions.When an MM accumulates rather than just making markets = strong signal.
Smart Money / WhalesExperienced traders with a track record of being early.The core of most top-tier content. Named wallets make any story compelling.
KOLsPublic figures. Their moves carry social weight.Spotting a KOL's wallet in a holder list = instant content.
RetailEveryday investors. Typically buy after the move.Retail entering = the move may be late. Smart money may be selling.
The Typical Flow

Foundations release tokens to VCs and founders (supply side) → Hedge funds and smart money buy early (demand side, early) → Price rises → Retail enters on the news (demand side, late) → Smart money starts selling.

TLDR

Supply side creates and distributes tokens. Demand side buys them. Smart money moves before retail. Retail moves on news. Nansen shows you where each player is positioned right now, and your job is to tell that story.

Glossary

Don't memorize all of these right now. Read through them once, then come back whenever a term comes up. This is a reference section, not a quiz.

Accumulation

When a wallet buys a token gradually, building a position. Often signals belief the price will rise. Opposite of distribution.

Distribution

When a wallet starts selling a previously accumulated token. Also called "taking profit." Often more valuable to catch than accumulation.

Cost Basis

The price at which a wallet originally bought. Tells you whether they're sitting on profit or loss, and how motivated they might be to sell.

Exchange Inflows / Outflows

Tokens moving to an exchange = preparing to sell. Tokens leaving an exchange = long-term holding. Outflows = less sell pressure.

Token Unlock

When a batch of vested tokens becomes available to move or sell. Known in advance. Creates natural 3-part content arcs.

Supply Concentration

When few wallets hold a large % of supply. Both a risk signal and a content angle. 5 wallets holding 40% = outsized price power.

Whale

A wallet moving $1M+ in a single transaction. No fixed definition, but large movers get this label.

Smart Money

Wallets with a track record of good trades. Often funds, market makers, or experienced traders. Nansen labels many of them.

Perpetuals / Perps

Betting on price direction without owning the token. Nansen tracks top perp traders by profit. Useful content angle.

uPNL

Unrealised Profit and Loss. How much a trader is up or down on an open position. $7M uPNL = $7M in paper profits.

Funding Rate

Periodic payment between longs and shorts. Very high = crowded trade = reversal risk. Worth mentioning in perp trader posts.

Alpha / Edge / Signal

The ability to act ahead of the crowd. For you: knowing something your audience doesn't, and being the one who surfaces it.

Cost Basis Motivation Matrix

This turns a single number into a narrative. Every time you check the Holders tab, ask: how motivated is this wallet to sell right now?

Profit LevelWhat It Usually MeansHow to Frame It
Up 0-2xStill building conviction, or recently entered. Low motivation to sell."Entered recently, holding through early gains."
Up 2-5xComfortable profit. Could start taking some off the table."Sitting on solid gains. Worth watching for early distribution."
Up 5-10xSignificant profit. Strong incentive to distribute."Up 8x on their position. Any sell activity is notable."
Up 10x+Very high likelihood of eventual distribution."Bought at $0.001, up 15x. When they sell, the market will feel it."
In lossCommitted long-term, or stuck. Check if they're buying more or inactive."Still underwater. Adding more = conviction. Silent = bag-holding."

Part 2 — Workflow 3

Exchange Flows: Reading the Supply Squeeze

Track when large amounts of a token move onto or off of exchanges. One of the most readable signals in Nansen because the direction tells the story.

Tokens Leave Exchange

Holder taking custody. "I'm not selling anytime soon." Less supply available = price tends to rise.

Tokens Enter Exchange

Holder positioning to sell. They put tokens there because they want the sell button.

Think of it like this: if you saw people walking into a pawn shop carrying gold, you'd expect prices to drop. People buying gold and walking out? Prices hold or rise.

What to look for

Sustained outflows over 48-72 hours are more meaningful than a single large withdrawal. Sudden large inflows after a price pump could signal early holders moving to sell into the rally. Outflows + smart money accumulation at the same time is a strong combination.

When the Signal Flips

During strong rallies: Inflows might be retail taking profits, not whales dumping. Check who's moving (retail-size wallets vs whale-size). After a crash: Outflows might be bag-holders giving up on selling, not conviction buying. Check cost basis. Market maker rebalancing: Normal operational transfers that look alarming but mean nothing.

Example Post

"Over the past 3 days, $40M of [token] has quietly left exchanges. That's supply leaving the market. No news. No hype. Just wallets moving tokens into cold storage."

Setting Up Smart Alerts

Configure Nansen to notify you automatically. The single most important feature for posting consistently without burning hours on research.

Wallet Alerts

"Notify me when this wallet transacts above $X." For wallets on your watchlist. When they move $2M into a new token, you know immediately.

Token Alerts

"Notify me when this token sees unusual activity from Smart Money, Funds, or Whales." For tokens you're monitoring.

Managing Alert Volume

Start with 3-5 alerts max. Set thresholds higher than you think. Goal: 1-3 meaningful notifications per day, not a constant stream. After a week, ask: "Did any alert lead to a post?" If not, adjust.

When an alert leads to a good post, mention it. "I got this alert at 9pm, one of the wallets I track just moved $3M into [token]. By morning, price was up 18%." This shows your audience you have a system.

Example Post

"One of the wallets I've been tracking just moved $2.5M into $TOKEN. This wallet was early on $PEPE and $ARB. When this wallet moves, I pay attention."

Part 2 — Reading Signals

Reading the Right Signals at the Right Time

Not all data is equally valuable at all times. Understanding when a signal matters most is part of developing your edge.

Accumulation (Before)

The most valuable and hardest to catch. Multiple wallets buying with no news or price action. The story: Something is happening before the crowd notices.

Confirmation (During)

Price is moving. You demonstrate Nansen caught it before. Named labels, accumulation that started pre-spike. The story: Here's who caused this.

Distribution (After)

Often overlooked, and the most actionable. When accumulators start selling. The story: Smart money is getting out.

Which of these three types of signal would you feel most comfortable posting about right now? Start there.

TLDR

Accumulation before the move = rarest and most valuable. Confirmation during = common but useful. Distribution after = underused and most actionable. Cover all three.

Your finger is hovering over the post button. Here's the framework that takes the guesswork out of it.

Three Questions That Turn Data Into a Story

On-chain data on its own means nothing to most people. Your job is to translate it into stakes.

1

What happened?

State the fact plainly. "$4.5M of PEPE was bought by 3 wallets in 6 hours." No interpretation yet.

2

Who did it?

"Two of those wallets are linked to Jump Trading." A named entity transforms the story. No label? Describe the pattern.

3

What does it mean for the reader?

"If you'd had a smart alert on these wallets, you'd have seen the signal 4 hours before." Make it personal.

Every piece of content should answer all three. If you can only answer one or two, find the missing data or hold the post.

Try this: write the answers to all three questions as single sentences before you write the actual post. If any sentence feels weak, that's the part of the story to strengthen.

Spotting Coordinated Wallet Activity

A single wallet buying is a data point. Multiple wallets buying the same token at the same time is a pattern. Patterns imply intent.

Identical Timing

3-5 wallets entering the same token within 30-60 minutes. That's not coincidence.

Matching Sizes

4 wallets each buy exactly $250K within hours. The uniformity is the story.

Common Funding

Coordinated wallets funded from the same source wallet. One entity, multiple addresses.

Repeated Pattern

Same cluster appeared in a previous token move. Not just coordination, a track record.

Example Post

"5 wallets moved into $TOKEN within a 40-minute window. Each bought $200K-$300K. Two funded from the same source. These aren't random buyers."

Ask yourself whenever you find an interesting wallet: "Does this wallet travel alone, or does it move with others?"

Validating Signals: Avoid Posting a Fake Move

Before you post, run the signal through this checklist. Takes 5 minutes. Saves your credibility.

✓

Check 1: Does the wallet have history?

Brand-new wallet + large coordinated buy = red flag. 6 months of diverse activity = more likely real.

✓

Check 2: Is the token legitimate?

Multiple DEXs? Real project? Sufficient liquidity to exit? If no, be very cautious.

✓

Check 3: Is there a social catalyst?

Did a KOL tweet about it 30 min before? Then those wallets are retail following a tip, not smart money.

✓

Check 4: Volume vs price action

$5M in "accumulation" but zero price movement? Could indicate wash trading.

✓

Check 5: What's the sell-side look like?

If one large wallet is distributing while coordinated wallets buy, you might be watching an exit strategy.

When in doubt

Frame as a question: "Interesting activity on $TOKEN. Is this coordinated accumulation or something else? Here's what I'm seeing." That framing protects you if the signal turns out to be manufactured.

Know Your Audience

The same insight will land very differently depending on who's reading it.

Crypto-Native Traders

Lead with the data point. Name the wallet immediately. Don't explain what a whale is. They respect evidence, not hype.

Crypto-Curious Investors

Lead with the outcome in plain English. Then explain how you found it. Use analogies. "Imagine seeing what a hedge fund buys before any filing."

Your Existing Audience

Match the tone they expect. Trading education account = tool that sharpens analysis. News account = source behind the story.

Growing from a Small Account

Reply to Trending Discourse

Jump in with data-backed replies to high-engagement tweets. "The on-chain data actually shows 3 whales accumulated $2M before this move."

Tag Relevant Entities

If Wintermute wallets are accumulating, tag Wintermute. A retweet from a large account can 10x your reach.

Keep Threads Short

A 7-tweet thread from a 2K account loses 80% by tweet 3. Max 3-4 tweets until engagement supports longer formats.

Engage Genuinely

Follow, reply to, and share content from other analysts. The crypto content community is small enough that genuine engagement gets noticed.

The "Not Financial Advice" Framework

You're a reporter, not a fortune teller. On-chain data shows behaviour, it does not predict outcomes.

Instead of...Say...
"This token is going to pump""Smart money is accumulating, a pattern that preceded moves before"
"Buy this now""Here's what the on-chain data is showing right now"
"This is dumping""Distribution starting from wallets that accumulated 3 weeks ago"
"Guaranteed to...""Historically, when this pattern appeared, [X] followed, but past patterns don't guarantee future outcomes"
When Someone DMs "Should I Buy This?"

Always some version of: "I don't give trade advice, but here's what the data shows, and you can decide from there." Point them to the Nansen feature to check themselves. Never tell someone to buy or sell, even casually, even in a DM.

Simple disclaimer: "On-chain analysis and observations. Not financial advice. I share what the data shows, you make your own decisions." Put it in your bio. That's enough.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Posting Without a Visual

A claim without evidence is just noise. Every post needs a chart or screenshot. No clean visual? Hold the post.

Burying the Lead

Lead with the most striking number or fact. Your audience decides in the first two seconds whether to keep reading.

Only Covering Accumulation

Distribution is underreported and genuinely useful. When accumulators sell, that's often a stronger post.

Leading with Nansen

Story first, tool second. "Whales accumulated $4.5M of PEPE" is the hook. "Track wallets on Nansen" is the follow-up.

Covering Everything at Once

5 signals today? Pick the strongest. Save the rest for tomorrow. You just bought 4 days of content.

What to Post When the Market Is Quiet

Scoring Your Watchlist: Smart Money Win Rates

Not all smart money is equally smart. Know which wallets are actually worth following.

Three Numbers Per Wallet

Win Rate

Out of last 10-20 positions, how many profitable? 70%+ = genuinely strong. Below 50% = noise.

Average Hold Time

Flip in 48 hours or hold for weeks? Short = post fast. Long = build episodic content.

Average Return

60% win rate with 5x avg wins > 80% win rate with 15% avg wins. The first takes calculated bets.

Building Beyond the Feed: Communities & Monetization

Understanding the trade-offs early helps you build content that doesn't close doors later.

Three Models

Pure Public

Everything free. Revenue from Nansen affiliate commissions only. Maximum reach, lowest friction.

Hybrid

Daily signals free on Twitter. Deepest research in paid Discord/Substack. Best for most affiliates at 20K+ followers.

Full Private

Teasers public, real analysis behind paywall. High per-member revenue but slow growth. 50K+ territory.

StageBest ModelWhy

Differentiate on depth, not exclusivity. Public = finished analysis. Private = process, reasoning, advance signals. Both feel valuable. Neither feels like leftovers.
